# Coercive and Controlling Behaviour (General Information)

Source note: General awareness information. NOT legal advice.

Coercive control describes a pattern of behaviour used to dominate or frighten another person and to strip away their freedom and independence. It is often gradual, which can make it hard to notice at first.

Signs that people commonly describe include:
- Being isolated from friends, family, or support networks.
- Having movements, phone, messages, or location monitored or tracked.
- Needing "permission" for everyday activities such as seeing people or spending money.
- Being subjected to threats, intimidation, or rules that change unpredictably.
- Having finances, documents, or transport controlled by another person.
- Being repeatedly put down so that confidence and self-esteem are worn away.

Because coercive control builds up over time, a person may start to doubt their own judgement. Naming the pattern can be an important first step.

In some countries coercive control is recognised in law; in others it is not. Either way, the impact on a person's wellbeing and safety is real and support is available.

If this resonates with your situation, a recognised support organisation can help you think through options safely and confidentially. This document is general information only and is not a substitute for professional advice.
