# Risk Assessment: Is This Dangerous?

## Factors that increase risk of serious harm or death

### Very high danger signs:

**Immediate threats:**
- Your abuser has threatened to hurt or kill you
- Your abuser has a weapon or has threatened weapon use
- Your abuser has physically hurt you seriously
- You're unable to breathe (strangling/choking)
- Your abuser has tried to hurt you before
- You feel terrified

**Escalating behavior:**
- The abuse is getting worse over time
- They hurt you when drunk or using drugs
- They hurt children or pets
- They've tried to hurt themselves or threatened suicide
- They say "if I can't have you, no one will"
- They become more controlling or jealous

**Loss of control:**
- They say they can't stop themselves
- They don't remember what they did
- They blame you for their violence
- They've never suffered consequences
- There's no pattern or trigger (violence seems random)

**Isolation:**
- They've cut you off from help
- No one knows what's happening
- You're not allowed to call police or go to hospital
- You have no phone or way to contact help

### High danger signs:

- Physical violence has happened (hitting, pushing, etc.)
- You've been to a hospital or doctor due to injuries
- They've used a weapon or threatened weapons
- Your children are being hurt or threatened
- They follow you or monitor you obsessively
- You're afraid they might kill you
- Substance abuse is involved
- They have anger management issues
- They were violent in previous relationships

### Moderate danger signs:

- Non-physical abuse (threats, controlling behavior, manipulation)
- Occasional shouting or intimidation
- Destroying property
- Threatening to hurt pets or take children
- Tracking you or checking on you
- Controlling your money or freedom
- Forcing sex or unwanted physical contact
- Emotional abuse (criticism, name-calling)

### Lower danger signs:

- One-time arguments or disagreements
- Yelling but no threats
- Emotional hurt but no control or violence
- Relationship conflict without abuse patterns

## The "Danger Assessment"

Answer YES or NO to these questions. More YES answers = higher danger:

1. Has your partner threatened to hurt or kill you?
2. Has your partner threatened to hurt or kill themselves?
3. Has your partner ever forced you to have sex when you didn't want to?
4. Does your partner become violent when drunk or using drugs?
5. Has your partner ever strangled, choked, or smothered you?
6. Are there weapons in your home?
7. Has your partner threatened you with a weapon?
8. Do you believe your partner is capable of killing you?
9. Is the abuse getting worse?
10. Has your partner injured you seriously (hospital-level)?
11. Have you been to a hospital or called police due to violence?
12. Does your partner have a criminal history?
13. Are your children being hurt or threatened?
14. Does your partner stalk or follow you?
15. Has your partner ever tried to strangle you?

**5 or more YES = HIGH RISK. Seek professional safety planning and consider leaving.**

## What increases risk when leaving

**Leaving can increase danger because:**
- Abusers often escalate when losing control
- They may try to prevent you from going
- They may use threats to get you back
- They may try to harm you or your children
- They may try to find you

**This is why safety planning is crucial when leaving.**

## Safety planning for high-risk situations

**If you're in immediate danger:**
- Call emergency services (999/911/000/112/119)
- Go to a public place
- Call a domestic abuse hotline
- Tell police you feel threatened

**Before you leave:**
1. Tell one trusted person
2. Pack a bag with essentials
3. Have money accessible
4. Know where you'll go (shelter, friend, family)
5. Plan your route and how you'll leave
6. Set a code word for help with a trusted friend
7. Have important documents (ID, birth certificate, medical records)
8. Memorize key phone numbers (abuser may take your phone)

**When you leave:**
- Leave when it's safest
- Tell someone where you're going
- Change phone number
- Don't leave a forwarding address if possible
- Tell your employer (for workplace safety)
- Get a restraining order if possible
- Contact police for welfare check if needed

**After you leave:**
- Vary your routine (don't go same places)
- Tell trusted people to watch for following
- Check for tracking apps/devices
- Keep your location private on social media
- Ask police for extra patrols if available
- Stay in contact with support services
- Have a backup plan if abuser finds you

## When to call emergency services

**Call 999 (UK) / 911 (US/Canada) / 000 (Australia) / 112 (EU) / 119 (Sri Lanka) if:**

- You're being hit, hurt, or attacked RIGHT NOW
- You can't breathe
- There's a weapon
- You've been seriously injured
- Your children are in danger
- You believe someone will be killed
- You're trapped and can't leave
- Your abuser is at your door and you're afraid

**It's never "not serious enough" to call.** Police take domestic abuse seriously.

## What happens after you call police

**Police will:**
- Respond quickly
- Separate you from the abuser if safe
- Document your statement
- Examine injuries and create photos
- Offer you information about support services
- Discuss options (charges, restraining order, safety planning)

**You can:**
- Request a female officer if needed
- Bring a trusted person to support you
- Ask for an interpreter if needed
- Request domestic abuse advocates be present
- Ask about next steps and options

## Support and assessment resources

**Professional risk assessment:**
- Domestic abuse organizations offer free assessments
- Police can assess your safety
- Shelters provide safety planning
- Therapists can help identify dangers
- Medical professionals can assess injuries
