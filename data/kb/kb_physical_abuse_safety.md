# Physical Abuse and Immediate Safety

## What is physical abuse?

Physical abuse includes:
- Hitting, punching, kicking, or slapping
- Pushing, shoving, or restraining
- Throwing objects at you
- Choking or strangling
- Threatening with weapons
- Hurting pets or children
- Destroying your property
- Any force used to control you

**Physical abuse is NEVER your fault and is ALWAYS wrong.**

## Signs of danger (immediate escalation risks)

**Seek emergency help immediately if:**
- Your partner has weapons (knife, gun, etc.)
- They've threatened to hurt or kill you
- They've hurt you seriously (broken bones, severe injuries)
- You can't breathe or are in severe pain
- You're afraid they might kill you
- They're preventing you from leaving
- They've strangled you (risk of sudden death)
- They're using drugs or alcohol before violence
- They've previously attempted to hurt themselves or you

## Immediate safety plan

**RIGHT NOW (if you're in danger):**
1. **Call emergency services** – 999 (UK), 911 (US/Canada), 000 (Australia), 112 (EU), 119 (Sri Lanka)
2. **Get to a safe place** – leave immediately if possible
3. **Go to a hospital** – even if injuries seem minor (creates medical record)
4. **Tell someone** – contact a friend, family member, or shelter

**Do NOT wait to see if it happens again.**

## Getting medical help

**At the hospital:**
- Tell them you were hit/hurt by a partner (they have protocols)
- Ask for photos of your injuries (for evidence)
- Request a full medical examination
- Get a written report of what they found
- Ask about injuries you might not feel yet

**Medical evidence is valuable for:**
- Police reports
- Restraining orders
- Custody decisions (if children involved)
- Future legal action
- Your safety planning

## Creating a safety plan

**Before leaving:**
- Pack a bag with documents (ID, birth certificate, passport)
- Hide cash if you can
- Memorize phone numbers of safe people
- Plan escape route from your home
- Arrange code word with trusted friend

**Important documents to save:**
- ID and passport
- Birth certificates (yours and any children's)
- Bank account information
- Proof of income
- Medical records
- Photos of injuries
- Police reports
- Custody/court orders (if applicable)

**When you leave:**
- Go to a shelter, friend, or family member
- Don't tell the abuser where you are
- Change your phone number
- Tell your employer for workplace safety
- Change passwords on all accounts
- Keep your phone with you always

## Restraining orders / protective orders

You can get a legal order that:
- Requires your abuser to stay away from you
- Forbids contact (phone, email, in person)
- Provides police backing if violated
- Can include custody arrangements
- Protects your children

**How to get one:**
- Contact your local court or police
- Apply for a restraining order
- Bring evidence (police reports, medical records, photos)
- Many are free or low-cost
- They're temporary initially but can be made permanent

## Long-term safety

**After leaving:**
- Join a support group for abuse survivors
- See a counselor or therapist
- Learn about building healthy relationships
- Plan how to stay safe long-term
- Change locks and security where you live
- Keep restraining order with you
- Tell trusted people to watch for safety threats

## Support resources

**Emergency:**
- Police emergency: 999 (UK), 911 (US/Canada), 000 (Australia), 112 (EU), 119 (Sri Lanka)

**24/7 Hotlines:**
- **UK:** National Domestic Violence Hotline – 0808 2000 247 (free, confidential, not on bill)
- **US:** National Domestic Violence Hotline – 1-800-799-7233
- **Australia:** 1800RESPECT – 1800 737 732
- **New Zealand:** Domestic Violence Action – 0800 456 450
- **Canada:** Canadian Domestic Violence Hotline – 1-877-222-6461
- **Sri Lanka:** Women's Centre – +94 11 2 697 625

**All calls are confidential. Text or chat online if you can't call safely.**
