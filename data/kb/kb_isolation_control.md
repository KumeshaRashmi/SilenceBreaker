# Isolation and Control: Recognizing Patterns

## What is isolation and control?

Isolation and control are forms of coercive control where someone:
- Prevents you from seeing family or friends
- Monitors your movements and phone
- Controls where you can go
- Restricts your independence and freedom
- Makes all decisions for you

## Common isolation tactics

**Social isolation:**
- Won't let you leave the house alone
- Cuts you off from friends and family
- Criticizes your relationships
- Prevents phone calls or contact with others
- Makes you ask permission to go out

**Financial control:**
- Controls all money and bank accounts
- Won't let you work or attend school
- Demands to know how money is spent
- Restricts access to resources
- Makes you completely dependent

**Movement restriction:**
- Tracks your location using GPS or apps
- Demands to know where you are at all times
- Gets angry if you go anywhere
- Uses threats to keep you home
- Threatens harm to you or loved ones if you leave

**Digital monitoring:**
- Checks your phone and emails constantly
- Installs tracking software
- Reads your messages and social media
- Won't let you have privacy
- Accuses you of cheating or lying based on monitoring

## Why this is abuse

Isolation and control are forms of psychological and emotional abuse. They:
- Strip away your independence
- Make you feel trapped and helpless
- Prevent you from seeking help
- Create financial dependency
- Are escalation risks—often accompany physical abuse

## What you can do

**Safety planning:**
- Save emergency numbers where you can access them (at work, memorized)
- Keep a small phone or device the abuser doesn't know about
- Stay in touch with one trusted person if possible
- Know the location of shelters and safe houses
- Have important documents in a safe place

**Documentation:**
- Keep a record of control incidents (dates, what happened)
- Save threatening messages as evidence
- Note times when you're not allowed to leave or have contact
- Photograph any evidence of monitoring

**Getting help:**
- Contact a domestic abuse hotline (they can help you safely)
- Speak to a trusted friend, family member, or counselor
- Ask for help at work or school (tell a manager/teacher privately)
- Visit a doctor and mention what's happening (creates medical record)
