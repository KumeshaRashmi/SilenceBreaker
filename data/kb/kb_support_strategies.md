# Coping Strategies and Emotional Support

## Immediate coping techniques

When you feel overwhelmed, scared, or angry, try these:

### Grounding techniques (5-4-3-2-1 method)

Engage your senses to bring yourself to the present moment:
- **5 things you see:** Name colors, shapes, objects around you
- **4 things you can touch:** Feel textures (soft, rough, warm, cold)
- **3 things you hear:** Notice sounds in your environment
- **2 things you smell:** Notice scents around you
- **1 thing you taste:** Gum, mints, or just notice your mouth

This takes about 2 minutes and helps when you're panicking or having flashbacks.

### Breathing exercises

**Box breathing (calms anxiety):**
1. Breathe in slowly for 4 counts
2. Hold for 4 counts
3. Breathe out slowly for 4 counts
4. Hold for 4 counts
5. Repeat 5-10 times

**4-7-8 breathing (helps sleep):**
1. Breathe in for 4 counts
2. Hold for 7 counts
3. Breathe out for 8 counts
4. Repeat 4 times

### Physical grounding

**When you feel numb or disconnected:**
- Hold ice in your hand (safe shock to bring you back)
- Splash cold water on your face
- Do jumping jacks or run in place
- Squeeze a stress ball hard
- Press your feet firmly on the ground
- Take a cold shower

**When you feel stuck or overwhelmed:**
- Go for a walk
- Dance to music you like
- Do stretching or yoga
- Take a bath with warm water
- Do any movement that feels good

## Managing difficult emotions

### Anger

Anger is valid—use it productively:
- Write angry letters (then burn or tear them up)
- Hit a pillow or punch a bag
- Scream into a pillow
- Run, exercise, or do intense physical activity
- Journal about your anger
- Talk to a trusted person or therapist

*Avoid:* Hurting yourself or others, or using it to make dangerous decisions

### Shame and guilt

Remember: **You are not to blame for the abuse.**

- The abuse is the abuser's responsibility
- You did nothing to deserve it
- Surviving is not shameful
- Ask yourself: Would I blame a friend in this situation?
- Practice self-compassion (talk to yourself like a good friend)
- Tell someone what you're feeling (shame grows in silence)

### Fear and anxiety

- Acknowledge the fear is real but separate from present danger
- Use grounding techniques
- Tell your support person you're afraid
- Make a safety plan to reduce uncertainty
- Exercise (reduces physical anxiety)
- Limit caffeine and alcohol
- Get adequate sleep

### Sadness and grief

You may grieve:
- The relationship you hoped for
- Time lost to abuse
- Damage to your self-worth
- Relationships that ended
- Safety you don't feel anymore

This grief is normal:
- Allow yourself to cry
- Talk about your loss
- Journal about your feelings
- Spend time in nature
- Express through art, music, or movement
- Connect with others who understand

## Building emotional resilience

### Self-care activities

**Daily self-care:**
- Shower and hygiene (helps you feel in control)
- Eat regular, nutritious meals
- Drink water (dehydration increases anxiety)
- Get enough sleep
- Move your body (walk, stretch, exercise)
- Spend time outside if safe

**Emotional self-care:**
- Do things you enjoy (hobbies, reading, gaming)
- Spend time with supportive people
- Set boundaries with others
- Say "no" to things that don't feel right
- Do acts of kindness for yourself
- Celebrate small wins

**Social self-care:**
- Call or text a trusted friend
- Join a support group
- Volunteer or help others
- Participate in activities you enjoy
- Make new friends who are supportive
- Rebuild relationships that were damaged

### Setting boundaries

Boundaries are rules you set for how people treat you:

**Examples:**
- "I won't accept yelling or name-calling"
- "I need space to process my feelings"
- "I won't tolerate controlling behavior"
- "I deserve respect and kindness"
- "I'm ending this conversation if you continue to..."

**How to set boundaries:**
1. Be clear about what you need
2. State it calmly (not angrily)
3. Follow through with consequences
4. Don't over-explain (you don't owe a reason)
5. Repeat if needed

### Building your support network

**Tell trusted people:**
- What happened (as much as you're comfortable sharing)
- What kind of support helps you
- What triggers your anxiety
- What you need going forward

**Quality over quantity:**
- One truly supportive person is better than many who don't understand
- Don't surround yourself with people who blame you
- Build relationships with others who are supportive

**Support groups:**
- Talking to survivors reduces isolation
- You learn you're not alone
- Others share coping strategies
- Available online and in-person
- Search for "abuse survivor support groups" in your area

## Managing triggers

Triggers are reminders of trauma:
- Certain places or smells
- Anniversaries of incidents
- Similar situations
- Loud noises
- Being alone or in crowds
- Specific words or behaviors

### Recognizing triggers

**Signs you've been triggered:**
- Sudden panic or fear
- Feeling like the abuse is happening now
- Freezing or feeling unable to move
- Anger that seems out of proportion
- Flashbacks or intrusive thoughts
- Physical symptoms (racing heart, sweating)

### Managing triggers

**When triggered:**
1. Remind yourself: "I'm safe now. That was then, this is now."
2. Use grounding techniques
3. Move to a safer space
4. Tell a trusted person
5. Do something self-soothing

**Reducing triggers over time:**
- Gradually expose yourself to triggers (with support)
- Therapy can help process triggers
- Replace negative associations with new ones
- Build new coping skills
- Most triggers become less intense with time

## Long-term emotional health

### Healing milestones

- Able to talk about what happened without extreme emotion
- Flashbacks become less frequent and intense
- Relationships feel safer
- You trust your own judgment again
- Able to enjoy activities you used to like
- Better sleep and fewer nightmares
- More stable mood and fewer extreme reactions
- Able to help others or find purpose

### When healing isn't linear

Healing isn't a straight line:
- Good days and bad days are normal
- Anniversaries or triggers can bring old feelings back
- This doesn't mean you've failed
- Recovery is still happening
- Reach out for support when you need it
