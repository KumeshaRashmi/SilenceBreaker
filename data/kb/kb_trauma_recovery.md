# Trauma, Healing, and Recovery

## Understanding trauma from abuse

Trauma from abuse can cause:
- Anxiety and panic attacks
- Depression and feelings of hopelessness
- Flashbacks to traumatic events
- Nightmares and sleep problems
- Physical reactions (pain, numbness)
- Difficulty trusting people
- Shame and self-blame
- Hypervigilance (always watching for danger)
- Emotional numbness

**This is normal. You're not broken. Your body and mind are protecting you.**

## Recognizing trauma responses

**Physical symptoms:**
- Headaches, muscle tension, chronic pain
- Stomach problems, nausea
- Dizziness or difficulty breathing
- Sleep problems or nightmares
- Fatigue and exhaustion
- Racing heartbeat or chest pain

**Emotional symptoms:**
- Overwhelming sadness or emptiness
- Anger or irritability
- Guilt or shame
- Anxiety and panic
- Emotional numbness or detachment
- Loss of interest in things you enjoyed

**Behavioral changes:**
- Avoidance of people or places
- Excessive vigilance (always alert)
- Difficulty concentrating
- Hyperactivity or inability to move
- Substance use or self-harm
- Isolation from friends and family

## Early recovery steps

**Immediate self-care:**
- Prioritize safety (physical and emotional)
- Get basic needs met (food, sleep, hygiene)
- Move your body (walk, stretch, exercise)
- Connect with one safe person
- Do grounding techniques (5 senses: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, etc.)
- Avoid major decisions for the first few weeks
- Be gentle with yourself

**Building safety:**
- Remove reminders of the abuser if possible
- Create a safe space in your home
- Tell people you trust what happened
- Keep support contacts visible
- Have a safety plan for if you feel triggered

**When you feel overwhelmed:**
- Ground yourself (ice on skin, splash cold water, focus on sounds)
- Breathing exercise (in for 4, hold for 4, out for 4)
- Call a crisis line or trusted person
- Journal or write out feelings
- Listen to music or calming sounds
- Go to a safe, public place if needed

## Getting professional help

**Types of therapy that help:**
- Trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT)
- Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)
- Group therapy with other survivors
- Individual counseling
- Psychiatric medication if needed

**Where to find help:**
- Your doctor (can refer you to therapists)
- Domestic abuse organizations (often have free counseling)
- Community mental health centers
- Private therapists (many offer sliding scale fees)
- Online therapy platforms (some cover trauma)
- Support groups for abuse survivors

**Finding an abuse-informed therapist:**
- Ask if they specialize in trauma
- Ensure they understand domestic abuse
- Check if they're trauma-informed
- Ask about their approach
- Don't settle for someone who doesn't feel right

## Long-term healing

**Weeks to months after leaving:**
- Continue therapy and support
- Rebuild routines and structure
- Reconnect with supportive people
- Join a support group
- Engage in activities you enjoy
- Set small goals and celebrate progress
- Be patient with recovery (it's not linear)

**Years after:**
- Trauma may resurface at times (anniversaries, triggers)
- Healing is a process, not a destination
- You can learn to manage triggers
- Relationships and trust can be rebuilt
- Many survivors thrive and build good lives

**Rebuilding trust:**
- Start with small, safe relationships
- Notice who is trustworthy and safe
- Take time before committing to new relationships
- Communicate about your needs
- Know that healthy relationships exist
- Your trauma doesn't define your future

## What helps healing

**Connection:**
- Spend time with supportive people
- Join a support group (in-person or online)
- Volunteer or help others
- Rebuild family relationships if safe
- Make new friendships

**Purpose:**
- Set goals (small and large)
- Learn new things
- Work on career or education
- Help other survivors
- Contribute to your community

**Wellness:**
- Regular exercise (releases endorphins)
- Nutritious eating
- Good sleep habits
- Mindfulness and meditation
- Time in nature
- Creative expression (art, music, writing)

**Processing the past:**
- Journaling
- Talking to a therapist
- Telling your story when ready
- Understanding how the abuse affected you
- Letting go of shame

## Support resources

**Crisis lines (24/7):**
- **UK:** National Domestic Violence Hotline – 0808 2000 247
- **US:** Crisis Text Line – Text HOME to 741741
- **Australia:** Lifeline – 13 11 14
- **Canada:** Crisis Text Line – Text HELLO to 741741
- **Sri Lanka:** Sri Lanka Helpline – 1006

**Counseling and therapy:**
- BetterHelp, Talkspace (online therapy)
- Psychology Today therapist finder
- Your local NHS (UK) or health service
- Community mental health centers
- Domestic abuse organizations (often free counseling)

**Support groups:**
- RAINN (Rape, Abuse & Incest National Network) – online/phone support
- Women's Aid support groups (UK)
- National Domestic Violence Hotline support resources
- Online forums and communities (verify they're moderated and safe)

**Recovery resources:**
- "Why Does He Do That?" by Lundy Bancroft
- "The Gift of Fear" by Gavin de Becker
- "Survivors" by John Bradshaw
- "The Body Keeps the Score" by Bessel van der Kolk
